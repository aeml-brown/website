Bone

Bone is a complex, anisotropic tissue. An anisotropic material has mechanical properties that are strongly dependent on the orientation at which the measurements are made. Another such anisotropic material is wood, which is easier to cut with the grain than against the grain. This is contrast to an isotropic material such as steel, which behaves in the same way regardless of orientation.

There are two basic kinds of bone tissue: compact cortical bone (which is solid and dense) and trabecular or cancellous bone (which is porous). The differences between these two tissue types contribute to the anisotropy of bone, which is apparent in the stress/strain graph below. Bone has greatest strength and stiffness along the longitudinal axis.

The compressive strength of compact bone is about 250 MPa,which is greater than that of concrete (approx. 4 MPa), wood (100 MPa) or glass (35-175 MPa), but less than that of steel (400-1500 MPa). It is about equal to cast iron (140-300 MPa), but at a much lower density, and therefore a great weight savings. Bone is not as strong in tension, at 150 MPa. The stiffness (elastic modulus) of cortical bone is between 17 and 25 GPa, which is close to that of wood.
